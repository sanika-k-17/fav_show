Holi

Diwali, the Festival of Lights, is celebrated with great enthusiasm across Maharashtra. It marks the victory of light over darkness and good over evil. Diwali begins with Vasu Baras and Dhanteras, followed by Narak Chaturdashi, Lakshmi Pujan, Padwa, and Bhaubeej, each day having cultural and spiritual significance. Homes are cleaned and decorated with diyas (oil lamps), colorful rangoli, and flowers. Firecrackers light up the night sky. On Lakshmi Pujan, families worship Goddess Lakshmi, the deity of wealth, for prosperity and well-being. Traditional sweets like karanji, chakli, laddu, and shankarpali are prepared and shared. Diwali also includes gifting, new clothes, and exchanging warm wishes. For Maharashtrians, Padwa is celebrated as Bali Pratipada, symbolizing the return of King Bali to earth, while Bhaubeej highlights the special bond between brothers and sisters. Diwali in Maharashtra is not just a religious festival; it‚Äôs a vibrant celebration of life, culture, and togetherness.

The festival celebrates Ganesha as the God of New Beginnings, the Remover of Obstacles and the God of Wisdom and Intelligence, and is observed throughout the Indian subcontinent by Hindus, especially in the states such as Maharashtra, Madhya Pradesh, Gujarat, Uttar Pradesh, Karnataka, Odisha, Telangana, Andhra Pradesh, Tamil Nadu, Kerala, and Goa, as well as Sri Lanka. Ganesh Chaturthi is also observed by the Hindu diaspora elsewhere such as in Australia, New Zealand, Canada, Singapore, Malaysia, Sri Lanka, Trinidad and Tobago, Guyana, Suriname, other parts of the Caribbean, Fiji, Mauritius, South Africa, the United States, and Europe. In the Gregorian calendar, Ganesh Chaturthi falls between 22 August and 20 September every year.

Krishna idols are bathed, dressed in new clothes, and placed in decorated cradles. Sweets like pedha, shrikhanda, and makkhan mishri are prepared and offered to the deity.
